Clinical Trial Design With Stress Test

1 Introduction

1.1 Notation

Consider a randomized Phase II clinical study that evaluates the efficacy of some medical intervention.

We confine our discussion to the cases where the overall intent-to-treat population \(S_C\) consists of two subgroups, \(S_1\) and \(S_2\), that are distinguished by the status of a binary biomarker.

Let \(j=1,2,C\) be a group index respectively denoting \(S_1\), \(S_2\) and the overall population \(S_C\). For each subject, we observe outcome \(Y_i\), treatment assignment \(T_i\) (\(T_i=0\) for control and \(1\) for treatment), and subgroup indicator (i.e. biomarker status) \(A_i\) (\(A_i=1,2\)). For notation convenience, we assume \(A_i = C\) holds for all \(i\).

Denote \(\delta_j\) the treatment effect size in group \(j\). That is, \[\delta_j = E(Y_i|T_i=1, A_i=j) - E(Y_i|T_i=0, A_i=j).\] Denote \(\pi_j = P(A_i =j)\) as the proportion of group \(j\) in the overall population (\(\pi_C=1\)). Then, \(\delta_C = \pi_1 \delta_1 + \pi_2 \delta_2\). Without loss of generality, we assume that the treatment effect is stronger in subgroup \(S_1\) than in \(S_2\); that is, \(\delta_1>\delta_2\) and consequently, \(\delta_1 > \delta_C\).

The goal is to evaluate if the treatment is effective for the subgroups \(S_1\) and \(S_2\) and for the overall population. Hence, there are three hypotheses of interest: \[H_{0,j}: \delta_j = 0 \quad for \quad j=1,2,C.\]

1.2 Study Design

Given the data generating distributions \(\scr{F}\), a valid study design \(\scr{D}\) is defined as a set of actions \(\scr{A}\) that satisfy certain constraints \(\scr{C}\). That is, \[\scr{D} = \{ \scr{A}; \scr{C}, \scr{F}\}.\]

The data generating distribution \(\scr{F}\) is a realization of the assumptions about how the data on the study are distributed. Data here often refer to the actual outcomes (i.e. \(Y_i\)) or its sufficient statistics (e.g. treatment effect \(\delta_j\)). In general, \(\scr{F}\) includes the probability assumptions for all the data that may occur on the study. These include baseline covariates, missingness, enrollment rate, subpopulation proportions etc.. The actions \(\scr{A}\) distinguish different designs. For example, the determination of sample size, number of interim analyses or stages, test statistics, efficacy and futility boundaries are all actions that a specific design needs to consider. The constraints \(\scr{C}\) are often defined with respect to the type I and type II error.

Give a loss function \(L\) (e.g. average sample size, mean squared error), the optimal design \(\scr{D}^*\) given \(\scr{F}\) and \(\scr{C}\) is the valid design that minimizes the loss. That is, \[ \scr{D}^* = \text{argmin}_{\scr{D}} L(\scr{D}; \scr{C}, \scr{F}). \]

1.3 Issues

At the design stage, the data generation distributions \(\scr{F}\) are often simplified in order to reduce the amount of computation work for constructing \(\scr{D}\), or even to ensure that a valid design \(\scr{D}\) exists. For example, it is common to assume that the variance of the treatment effect size is known or the data are missing completely at random if there is missingness. Nevertheless, the consequences when these assumptions about \(\scr{F}\) are inaccurate are largely ignored in practice. In our opinion, this is one of the major causes for study failures in clinical trials.

1.4 Goal

In this paper, we conduct simulation studies to evaluate the impact of the misspecification of data generation distributions at the design stage. We evaluate different study designs in the biomarker-efficacy study settings. We also propose a general strategy for incorporating the uncertainties in the data generation distributions to improve the robustness of candidate study designs.

2 A Motivation Example

Consider a single stage study. At the design stage, in order to specify \(\scr{F}\), we assume that

  • \(\pi_1 = \pi^*\)
  • \(Y_i|T_i=t, A_i=j \sim N(\mu_{t,j}, \sigma^{2})\) for \(j=1,2\).

For the constraints \(\scr{C}\), we consider the family-wise error rate \(\alpha\) under the global null hypothesis \(H_0: \delta_1 = \delta_2 = 0\) and the power constraint such that the minimum individual power is at least \(\beta\).

We consider several sets of actions \(\scr{A}\) for the family-wise type I error rate control:

  • We apply Bonferroni adjustment by equally splitting \(\alpha\) among hypotheses \(H_{0,j}\) (\(j=0,1,C\)).
  • We apply Bonferroni adjustment by splitting \(\alpha\) arbitrarily among hypotheses \(H_{0,j}\) such that \(\alpha_{1} + \alpha_{2} + \alpha_{C} = \alpha\).

We then power the study with sample size \(N\) for the power constraints.

Let \(L\) be the sample size. Then the optimal design \(\scr{D}^*\) will be the valid design that gives the smallest sample size.

Next, we will evaluate the robustness of \(\scr{D}^*\) when \(\scr{F}\) is misspecified. Specifically, we assume that in the true distribution \(\scr{F}'\), \[ \pi_1 \sim Unif(\pi^* - \Delta, \pi^* + \Delta). \]

As metrics of robustness, we consider bias, mean squared error and standard deviation of \(\delta_j\) as well as the power of the three hypothesis. We report the worst case and the average case of these metrics with respect to \(\scr{F}'\).
